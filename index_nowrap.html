Keeping the
doors open

Wimberley's shops and resorts are slowly bouncing back
from the Memorial Day weekend floods

Story by Sean Collins Walsh
Title photo by Shelby Tauber
Videos by Kelly West
Presentation by Paighten Harkins

John McNeal has been coming to the 7A Resort in Wimberley for eight years. In recent summers, he began telling his girlfriend that he wanted to do something more than float on the Blanco River and sip beers during their annual Hill Country pilgrimage.

“I pestered her for years — ‘I’m going to marry you in that river’ — and lo and behold, I asked the JP and he said, ‘Yeah, no problem,’ ” said McNeal, 43, who lives in Channelview, near Houston. “We were married right at the point of that rock last year.”

A year later, they returned to find their paradise a war zone, with skinned and uprooted Cypress trees lining banks of the river, collapsed houses dotting River Road and cleanup crews outnumbering vacationers.

“It was a little heartbreaking, really, and then coming down the road and knowing that we’ve seen this here and that there — and it’s just gone,” McNeal said. “My wife just couldn’t take it anymore. She just started crying when we were checking in.”

Neal was one of the few 7A loyalists who were able to return this summer. The resort lost 15 of its 20 cabins and its pool, said Carroll Czichos, a co-owner of the resort and Wimberley’s fire chief. The property did not have flood insurance, and rebuilding could cost $300,000 at a time when they are still returning deposits to vacationers whose reservations they had to cancel, he said.

As the Hill Country tourist town begins to bounce back from the Memorial Day weekend floods, business in Wimberley has picked up considerably since the immediate aftermath of the disaster, officials and shopkeepers said. But traffic is still well below what it usually is during the summer, and many believe it’s because 7A and Rio Benito, the other big resort in town, are still recovering from the damage.

“There were thousands of people planning to come in Wimberley and stay in those larger places, and they’re either trying to find other places or just canceled their reservations altogether. That has had an effect,” said Cathy Moreman, executive director of the Wimberley Chamber of Commerce.

Data on sales-tax revenue from this summer are not yet available, City Administrator Don Ferguson said. But anecdotal evidence suggests that June was devastatingly slow for many businesses, but picked up some in July. Restaurants have been doing better than retailers.

The flooding killed 12 people in Central Texas, and two are still missing. It took out all but one bridge in the Wimberley area and damaged 400 homes. But the destruction was limited to the banks of the Blanco River, and the main shopping area along Cypress Creek suffered almost no damage.

Despite an advertising campaign by her group and a promotional visit from Gov. Greg Abbott, Moreman said Wimberley is still fighting the perception that the flood wiped the town off the map.

“Many people still think that Wimberley was, if not completely washed away, at least damaged to the point where it’s not worth visiting. But of course that’s not true,” she said.

Two months after devastating flood waters rose more than 40 feet, the Blanco River looks calm, clean and inviting to guests of the 7A Resort in Wimberley. The flood uprooted Cypress trees that were hundreds of years old and stripped away the undergrowth in the river. “It’s almost taken the river banks to bare bones,” said Trish Czichos, co-owner of the resort. Keeping
afloat

Two women chat at the Wimberley Cafe on July 29. Many Wimberley businesses are struggling to stay busy because of a lack of tourists after the Memorial Day floods. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

At lunchtime Wednesday, restaurants in Wimberley Square were almost full, but many of the quirky shops and boutiques that Wimberley is famous for were almost empty.

The staff at Cedar Chest created a Wimberley Strong Pandora Charm to raise money for the flood victims and community affected by the Memorial Day floods. Selling the charms raised $8,000 to aid the community. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

At the eclectic Old Mill Store, where Abbott shopped this summer to spread the word that Wimberley is open for business, assistant manager Amy Jordan fixed a turkey sandwich for a customer and looked out at the otherwise empty store.

“Abbott was here. That might’ve helped some, I’d like to think. But not too much,” Jordan, 60, said. “Summers past, it’s just been crazy in here.”

At Cedar Chest, which sells jewelry and women’s wear, the steady stream of customers belied the struggles facing Sarah Barlow, the owner. She pointed to racks of clothes from the spring season that she is discounting for 50 percent to make room for the fall inventory.

Makaela Goss, 7, pets a cat at Wall Street Western in Wimberley on July 29. The five cats at the store can be photographed for $5, which is donated to flood relief in the city. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

“We lost our spring this year. We have to keep up with the seasons in retail,” Barlow said. The business is operating under a “caution flag,” she said, meaning it won’t bring in new inventory until it unloads the stuff it has.

One item she has had no problem selling is a $55 Pandora bracelet charm with the shape of Texas and “Wimberley Strong” engraved on it. Barlow is sending the profits to 7A and Rio Benito, a decision that drew criticism from some who thought aid should go to displaced homeowners rather than businesses, she said.

But Barlow has her reasons: If the resorts suffer, the shops suffer. And if the shops suffer, the city government — which has no property or hotel tax and relies on a 1 percent sales tax — also suffers.

“Business helps support this economy,” she said. “If people don’t shop here, we will need money to run the city.”

Looking to
rebuild

Trish Czichos, co-owner of 7A Resort and manager of Pioneer Town in Wimberley, on July 29. The resort was hit hard by the Memorial Day weekend floods. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

The Czichos family in 1946 began renting cabins on the Blanco River on a 7.45-acre plot, which is why the 115-acre operation is now called “7A.” Several years later, they opened Pioneer Town.

The base of a cabin in the 7A Resort sits near the Blanco River. Many of the cabins were swept away or severely damaged by the Memorial Day floods. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

One summer, a “strutter” performing in a dance routine at a Pioneer Town show caught the eye of Raymond Czichos, the founder’s son. They married, and now Trish Czichos is the manager of the Pioneer Town.

During the Memorial Day weekend floods, Pioneer Town became a refugee camp of sorts for the 200 vacationers staying at the resort. The water swept away four cabins and damaged almost all of them.

“Basically our biggest blessing is that we got everyone out,” she said.

Some of 7A’s cabins were in the federally designated floodplain where property owners must get flood insurance. But they were built decades before the insurance program was instituted and were grandfathered in. The family didn’t opt to get insurance anyway, Carroll Czichos said, because flood waters had never reached as high as they did over Memorial Day weekend.

Lux Toomey, 5, has her hair braided by her aunt, Nicole Toomey, at the 7A Resort in Wimberley on July 29. Nicole originally rented out Cabin 8 in August, but the Memorial Day floods swept away the building. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

The family is exploring options on how to rebuild and is requesting permission from the city to build an RV park.

Trish Czichos said she believes the resort will recover, although it won’t be exactly the same. It’s the same way she feels about Wimberley when she drives by the vanished homes of people she knows.

“It’s almost as if you see all the blank areas and you go, ‘Were they really there? There’s a kind of disbelief that that could have happened and that could have been so powerful,” she said. “There’s a sadness.”

The damage is clear on the banks of the Blanco River from the Memorial Day floods at the 7A Resort. (Shelby Tauber/American-Statesman)

The floods

A youth looks into Blanco River floodwaters in Wimberley. Overnight, the waters ripped the home behind him from its foundation. (Rodolfo Gonzalez/American-Statesman)

The Memorial Day weekend floods ravaged Wimberley and the surrounding areas, killing at least 12 people and doing millions of dollars of damage to Central Texas. An early estimate totaled $33 million in Hays County, home to hard hit areas like Wimberley and San Marcos, alone.
